Historical context
Independent newspaper like the New York Call and the Jewish Daily Forward largely helped to spread Union messages.
On November 22nd, 1909, hundreds of union leaders and members piled into the Cooper Union Great Hall to discuss the strikes of female garment workers in New York City, and the possibility of a city-wide, general strike.
Max Blanck and Isaac Harris, owners of the triangle shirtwaist factory. Hired prostitutes and thugs to harass and attack striking workers.

I ask the sanitary inspector to come; the sanitary conditions are so bad. You take a drink of the water that is provided for the girls to drink. It makes you sick all day! The health inspector comes. He makes the boss have the place cleaned - that is, once. Next week it is as bad as ever. If the boss finds out who sent for the health inspector, I am discharged.”

-Clara Lemlich, Good Housekeeping
Samuel Gompers was the president of the AFL.
Samuel Gompers
Among the important figures in Cooper Hall was Samuel Gompers. Samuel Gompers was the president of the American Federation of Labor. He was one of the leading adversaries against the Women’s Trade Union League, ignoring their attempts to cultivate ties with the AFL.

Yes, Mr. Shirtwaist Manufacturer, it may be inconvenient for you if your boys and girls go out on strike, but there are things of more importance than your convenience and your profit. There are the lives of the boys and girls working in your business.”

-Samuel Gompers

In the beginning there was resistance in the labor movement in general against women organizing. The unions were trying to advance the idea that men deserved a family wage. Because they, under this theory, needed to support a family, they should receive more money. But if women entered as well it was harder to make that argument. Also, the belief at the time was that women would only be in the labor market until they married.”

-Dr. Annie Polland, Executive Director of the American Jewish Historical Society
Rose Schneiderman speaking at a union rally, circa 1910
Rose Schneiderman
Also in attendance was Rose Schneiderman. One of the most important leaders of working women and labor organizers in America, Rose Schneiderman was Vice President of the Women’s Trade Labor Union (WTLU)'s New York branch. She helped to organize this town hall and came out in full support of a general strike.

All the time our union was progressing very nicely. There were lectures to make us understand what trades unionism is and our real position in the labor movement."

-Rose Schneiderman
Clara Lemlich with a caption reading: “The soul of this young women’s revolution, a spirit of fire and tears” in The Survey, 1909.
Clara Lemlich
Clara Lemlich, a twenty-three year old young Jewish Woman, organized the International Ladies’ Garment Workers’ Union. An ardent supporter of the strike, she had spent most of her teenage years fighting for women's trade unions and organizing strikes in Garment Factories. Tired of only talk, she usurped the expected speakers and with the support of the crowd behind her, demanded that the union leadership declare a general strike.

Each one talked about the terrible conditions of the workers in the shop. But no one gave any practical or valid solution.”

-Clara Lemlich
“Portrait of women shirtwaist strikers holding copies of ‘The Call’ A placard with Hebrew writing hands in the background,” 1910. The Kheel Center for Labor-Management Documentation and Archives, Cornell University.

...the soul of this young women’s revolution is Clara Lemlich a spirit of fire and tears, devoid of egotism, unable to tolerate the thought of human suffering.”

-Mary Brown Sumner, The Survey, 1909.

I read about them all now - all those important people and Clara Lemlich here, Clara Lemlich there! What did I know about trade unionism? Audacity - that was all I had - audacity!”

-Clara Lemlich in a 1954 interview
Hesitantly, the union leaders accepted and declared a general strike. Benjamin Feigenbaum, a prominent union journalist, asked the members of the audience and those on stage to take a traditional Jewish oath:
“If I forget thee oh Jerusalem, may my right hand wither, may my tongue forget it’s speech.”
The word Jerusalem was replaced with union.
Samuel Gompers and other male union leaders like him did not understand the importance of negotiating safety conditions in female workplaces.
