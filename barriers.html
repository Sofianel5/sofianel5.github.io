Above is an interview conducted by historian Joan Morrison where she interviews Pauline Newman, a Triangle Shirtwaist Factory worker. She describes her experiences at the factory.
As immigrants flooded into New York City at the turn of the century, poor immigrant women, particularly Russian Jews, saw employment in garment factories as cheap labourers. However, the treatment they experienced went beyond poor pay; it was tantamount to opression.

Pauline Newman’s story is just one of thousands of girls working in the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory. They were subjected to long hours hile being paid starvation wages in workplaces prone to violent accidents and abuses.

Elizabeth, who usually worked next to me, no longer is here, for just yesterday she had got her fingers caught in the textile machine, and now her fingers are all mangled and bloody, they were basically useless. Helen has taken her place, but Helen got beat today because she accidentally tripped and fell in the factory.”

The rise of laissez faire capitalism and Social Darwinism in the 18th and 19th century led to the belief that many of the poor, struggling workers were only in their position because of a personal fault, and that they do not deserve to receive intervention.

There’s no government intervention. There’s no labor legislation. There’s really very little sanitary legislation protecting the health and safety of workers. There are no minimum wage laws or maximum hour laws. Nobody’s watching”

-Steve Fraser

The law of competition may be sometimes hard for the individual, [but] it is best for the race , because it insures the survival of the fittest in every department."

-Andrew Carnegie (1889)
An image of Joseph Lochner
This belief influenced even the Supreme Court. In Lochner vs. New York, the court ruled in favor of an employer, Lochner, who claimed that the NY Legislature’s Bakeshop Act of 1985, which helped to regulate the conditions by prohibiting bakers from working more than 10 hours a day, was unconstitutional. This set the precedent for future laws advocating the rights of workers to be struck down.

Without any laws set in place to regulate wages, hour, or treatment, workers, especially women, were prone to verbal, physical and sexual abuse. Because of this, beginning in the late 19th century, workers began to organize into unions to protect their rights.

Clearly, garment workers were not ‘enslaved,’ as African slaves had been in America. Garment workers used the term a powerful metaphor to drive home both their powerlessness and their struggles for (industrial) freedom. Lemlich knew in her heart that, like slaves, garment workers too would have their emancipation proclamation someday. She hoped, as others did, that they could free themselves.”

-Richard A. Greenwald, The Triangle Fire, Protocols of Peace, and Industrial Democracy in Progressive Era New York
