Above is an interview conducted by historian Joan Morrison where she interviews Pauline Newman, a Triangle Shirtwaist Factory worker. She describes her experiences at the factory.
During the Industrial Revolution, young girls began leaving their homes to work in factories, enticed by the idea of freedom and being able to earn their own wages. However, these girls soon realized that what awaited them was far from what they dreamed of.
Pauline Newman’s story is just one of thousands of girls working in the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory. They were subjected to long hours and horrible conditions, yet were being paid vastly insufficient wages. Easily replaceable and readily discarded, there were always more applicants ready to take their place.

Elizabeth, who usually worked next to me, no longer is here, for just yesterday she had got her fingers caught in the textile machine, and now her fingers are all mangled and bloody, they were basically useless. Helen has taken her place, but Helen got beat today because she accidentally tripped and fell in the factory.”

The rise of Laissez-Faire capitalism, the belief in the economy's ability to flurish in the absence of government interference, was also an obstacle in the fight for reform. The theory of social Darwinism supported that only the strongest, most resilient businesses would thrive. These beliefs captured the zeitgeist during the 18th and 19th century, greatly hindering any possible progress reform movements could have made.

Without any laws to regulate the treatment of workers, verbal, physical, financial, and sexually abuse was commonplace. Their wages could be deducted for arbirary reasons without the workers being able to fight back. These factors ultimately pushed the workers over the edge, leading to their strike: the Uprising of the 20,000.

This belief spread all the way up to the Supreme Court, in its ruling of the Lochner vs. New York Case. Lochner was an employer who claimed that the NY Legislature’s Bakeshop Act of 1985, which helped to regulate the conditions and working time in bakeries, prohibiting them from working more than 10 hours a day, was unconstitutional. The Supreme Court ruled in favor of Lochner, setting the precedent for future laws advocating the rights of workers to be struck down.
