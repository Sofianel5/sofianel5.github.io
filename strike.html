The Strike

“It was not a women’s fancy that drove them to it. But an eruption of a long smoldering volcano, an overflow of suffering, abuse and exhaustion.”

-Theresa Malkiel, The New York Call
Women garment workers on strike
The brutal beatings that strikers like Clara Lemlich (above) sustained only strengthened the solidarity of the young activists.
Following the declaration of a general strike, 15,000 waistmakers went on strike across New York City. This was not a spontaneous event. For 8 years before the strike, Clara Lemlich had been organizing an attempt to prove to the leadership of the AFL and ILGWU that a general strike was possible in New York City.

The momentum these strikers had gained in spite of the persecution caused Lemlich and other labor leaders to push for a general strike. However, violence pushed the national IGLWU leaders into considered calling off the strike. The male leadership of the AFL and ILGWU, John Dyche and Samuel Gompers, also resisted the idea of the general strike because they did not believe that the teenage girls could sustain one.

However, Lemlich’s inspiring Cooper Union speech, broke the barriers set up by male-dominated unions to secure support for a women-led general strike which had never been seen before.

I can't talk fellowship to you who are gathered here [middle class WTUL members]. Too much blood has been spilled. I know from my experience it is up to the working people to save themselves. The only way they [women workers] can save themselves is by a strong working-class movement.”

-Rose Schneirderman, April 1911

Years of preparation had created an infrastructure strong enough to withstand and counteract employers who did everything the could to divide the strikers: stirring up ethnic animosities, attacking the woman's virtue, and when all else failed, unleashing physical violence.”

Annelise Orleck, Common Sense and a Little Fire
Female strikers pose for a photo, 1909.
The 1909 strike fanned the flames of female-led labour strikes across the northeast and midwest. Within weeks of the uprising, 15,000 women in philadelphia walked off their jobs. In the next 4 years in Chicago, stikes followed in Cleveland, Iowa, and Brooklyn. It’s unlikely that unionism would’ve expanded in powerful the way it did without these women.

The events of the strike led to advancements for women in unions. Women, hardly recognized in unions before the uprising, ended the strike with 50% membership. This breathed new life into urban labor movements.

Certainly, in order to sustain the months long strike they needed the help of union men, Socialist Party women, the Women’s Trade Union League, and upper-class suffragists. Nevertheless, this was a genuine grassroots protest, sparked, defined, and led by working women. Shop-floor leaders, many still in their teens, were responsible both for the size of the strike and the singular ‘spirit of the strikers.’ They had inspired their allies, not the reverse.”

-Annelise Orleck, Common Sense and a Little Fire
The 1909 uprising fanned the flames of strikes in the coming years, such as this one in Union Square sometime between 1915 and 1920.
However, the status quo in employment largely continued after the end of the strike. Though the strike broke barriers in labor activism, it had mixed success for the participating workers. While some women won pay increases and union recognition, many didn’t.
More tragically, the male ILGWU negotiators ended the strike without consulting the strikers, deciding that safety conditions were not as important as other issues. The legacy of this would come back to haunt the labour movement when in 1911, the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory burned.
In this way, the Uprising of the 20,000 was not an ultimate success for the labor movement, but was instead a truly boundary-pushing moment for women workers that would guide the movement for years to come.

The working woman needs bread, but she needs roses, too”

-Rose Schneirderman

The belief at the time was that women would only be in the labor market until they married. Because they were short-term workers it wasn't worthwhile to organize them. That is why the 1909 strike was so important--led by women, it proved their dedication to the union cause.”

-Annie Polland, Ph.D, Executive Director of the American Jewish Historical Society
